The Story

A village that moved itself, and the town that invented the circus

In 1893 the New York Times headline read “Destruction to Katonah.” New York City had condemned the village to flood the valley for its Croton reservoir system. The residents voted not to scatter but to move: some 55 homes, stores, and a church were hauled to higher ground on soaped rails and log rollers, families sometimes living inside en route. The relocated village was laid out by the Olmsted firm with deed rules separating commerce from homes, proto-zoning two decades before New York had a zoning law. That planned village is today’s Katonah Village Historic District of Victorian “painted ladies,” on the National Register since 1983.

Somers has its own claim to fame: the cradle of the American circus. Around 1805, farmer Hachaliah Bailey brought an elephant named Old Bet to town, and the crowds she drew turned a farm animal into a touring business that seeded the American traveling menagerie. Bailey’s Elephant Hotel (1820s) still anchors the crossroads of Routes 100 and 202, a National Historic Landmark that now serves as Town Hall and houses the Museum of the Early American Circus.

One Katonah dirt road is home to “a touring jazz guitarist, an Academy Award-winning animator, the lighting designer for the New York City Ballet... and the puppeteer behind Ernie on Sesame Street.”
— Upstate House

The neighbors match the setting. Martha Stewart farms the 150-acre Cantitoe Corners estate in Katonah. George Soros keeps an estate here. Stanley Tucci grew up in town; Garry Trudeau went to the Harvey School; former US Poet Laureate Billy Collins leads the Katonah Poetry Series at the Village Library. Ralph Lauren and Richard Gere (co-owner of the Bedford Post Inn) are one town over in Bedford; Blake Lively and Ryan Reynolds are nearby in Pound Ridge. Two more National Historic Landmarks sit in the hamlet: John Jay’s homestead and Stepping Stones, the home of AA cofounders Bill and Lois Wilson.

Downtown, or not: you get both

Katonah hamlet, ten minutes away, is the walkable rail-town downtown: nearly every storefront on Katonah Avenue is independently owned, anchored by the Katonah Museum of Art, the Village Library, the 138-year-old Kelloggs & Lawrence hardware store, and Caramoor, the 90-acre estate whose summer music festival has run since 1945. Somers itself has no traditional downtown; shopping concentrates at Somers Commons, and the civic heart is the Elephant Hotel. Farmers markets run Saturdays at the John Jay Homestead and Sundays at Muscoot Farm, which is effectively next door to this house.

Straight Talk

What a careful buyer verifies

Every house has tradeoffs. Here are this one’s, and the checks to run before contract.

  • Status is Pending. The listing went pending in mid-2026; confirm availability or backup-offer position with the listing agent first.
  • Well and septic. Normal for the area, but budget for it: Westchester law requires a well test at sale (~$425–$583), and septic wants a pump-out every 3–5 years (~$300–$500). Ask for the septic’s age, location, and last service.
  • Heating fuel. The gas range on a rural well/septic road almost certainly means propane. Verify tank ownership vs lease and annual fuel costs; radiant hot-water heat is comfortable but fuel-fed.
  • Electricity costs. NYSEG territory runs ~29–31¢/kWh all-in, roughly 60% above the US average. Ask for 12 months of utility bills on a 6,000 sq ft house.
  • Schools tier. Somers is a strong A district with elite resources and athletics; if a top-25 NY academic ranking is the hard requirement, that is Chappaqua or Katonah-Lewisboro territory at a housing premium.
  • Commute reality. Rail parking at Katonah favors Bedford residents ($1,341/yr for others); plan on the Goldens Bridge lot or daily meters. Drive times quoted are traffic-free.
  • Cell signal. Coverage is strong on paper, but hilly northern Westchester has dead spots; test all household carriers at the house during a showing.
  • Road position. Pines Bridge Road (Route 139) is a through road; walk the 1.87 acres to judge setback, buffer, and road noise at commute hour.
